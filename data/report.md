# British & Boer Troop Movements in the Eastern Cape — Second Boer War (Oct 1899 – May 1902)

*A source-cited synthesis. Every statement below is tied to a verified claim (adversarial 3-vote
verification, ≥2/3 to confirm). Confidence is marked. Figures and routes that **failed** verification
are listed in §5 so they are not mistaken for fact. Generated 2026-06-12 from public sources.*

---

## 1. The strategic frame — why British movements were *reactive*

British troop movements in the Eastern Cape were, throughout the war, **largely reactive to Boer
initiative**. A general uprising of the Cape's Dutch-speaking population was a deliberate, central
Boer strategic objective — "regarded by many Boer leaders as the key to their success" — but it
never materialised on a war-winning scale. Where risings did occur (mainly *west* of the
Cape Town–Johannesburg railway) they were strategically unimportant or were suppressed by
geography and Boer command problems. The British therefore spent the war **chasing** Boer
incursions rather than mounting their own offensive scheme of manoeuvre in this theatre.
*(Confidence: high — UCT scholarly thesis + mainstream historiography. [1][2])*

The campaign falls into two clear phases.

## 2. Phase 1 — the conventional invasion & Stormberg (Nov–Dec 1899)

**Boer side.** Orange Free State commandos under **Gen. Jan Hendrik Olivier** (commanding the
Rouxville and Thaba Nchu commandos) crossed the Orange River and seized the north-eastern Cape
frontier: **Aliwal North fell on 13 November 1899**, and the Boers occupied **Stormberg Junction**,
a key rail node. Olivier's forces moved through the Colesberg, Barkly East and Dordrecht districts.
Olivier was the senior Boer commander at Stormberg. *(Confidence: high. [3][4][5][6])*

**British side.** **Lt-Gen Sir William Gatacre** (3rd Division) attempted to retake Stormberg. He
moved ~**2,600 men with 12 guns** by rail to **Molteno** (the nearest railhead still in British
hands), then launched a **night march** (begun ~9–9.15 pm, 9 Dec) intending to storm the
**Kissieberg** hill above the Boer position. Named units: **2nd Northumberland Fusiliers, 2nd Royal
Irish Rifles, mounted infantry, Royal Engineers, and the 74th & 77th Field Batteries**.
*(Confidence: high. [4][5][6])*

**Outcome.** The night march failed: the locally engaged guides became disorientated, the column
lost its way (reaching a colliery railway line ~12.30 am where Gatacre realised the turning had been
missed, arriving under the Kissieberg further north than planned ~4.20 am), compounded by a
last-minute route change and exhausted troops. The result was a **Boer victory** — **13 officers and
548 men captured** (plus killed and wounded; at least one gun lost in boggy ground) — making
**Stormberg one of the three "Black Week" defeats** alongside Magersfontein (11 Dec) and Colenso
(15 Dec). *(Confidence: high. [4][5][6])*

## 2a. Phase 1b — the Colesberg front (Dec 1899 – Feb 1900)  *(added from* With the Flag to Pretoria*)*

West of Stormberg, a second conventional front opened around **Colesberg**, where **OFS commandos
(Gen. Schoeman)** had crossed at **Norval's Pont** in November 1899 (~2,000 men) and occupied the
district, threatening the midland railway. **Maj-Gen John French**, with just under 3,000 men (6th
Dragoon Guards, 10th Hussars, NSW Lancers, NZ Mounted Infantry, O & R Batteries RHA, the Berkshires),
fought a mobile, weeks-long containment campaign from **Arundel** and **Rensburg**:
- **13 Dec 1899** — French repels a ~1,800-strong Boer attack near **Arundel/Kuilfontein** (~40 Boer
  casualties).
- **29 Dec 1899 – 1 Jan 1900** — Boers fall back to Colesberg; French advances to Rensburg, seizes
  Porter's Hill and a kopje by **Coles Kop**.
- **4 Jan 1900** — Schoeman's night outflanking move is trapped and repulsed (~90 Boer casualties, 21
  prisoners; **Maj Harvey** killed).
- **6 Jan 1900** — the **Suffolks disaster**: a night attack on **Grassy/Suffolk Hill** is repulsed,
  ~200 of two companies killed/wounded/captured and **Lt-Col Watson** killed.
- **Jan–Feb 1900** — British hold **Slingersfontein** (Australian/New Zealand marksmanship noted), but
  when **French is summoned to relieve Kimberley (29 Jan)** and **Clements** takes over, the weakened
  force abandons Coles Kop and Slingersfontein, falls back on Arundel, and the **Boers reoccupy
  Colesberg** in February. *(Confidence: high, exact kopje coordinates approximate. [30])*

## 3. Phase 2 — the guerrilla invasions of the Cape (Dec 1900 – 1902)

From late 1900 the Boers sent commandos deep into the Cape Midlands and frontier to raise the
country. British columns and the expanding **blockhouse / railway lines** responded by chasing,
cornering and capturing them.

**Kritzinger — first invasion (Dec 1900 – 29 Apr 1901).** Commandant **P.H. Kritzinger** led a
long looping raid: **Venterstad → Steynsburg → Stormberg → New Bethesda → Richmond → Murraysburg
→ Aberdeen → Willowmore → Oudtshoorn → Uniondale → Mossel Bay**, then back via **Somerset East,
Bedford, Tarkastad and Cradock**, leaving the Cape on **29 April 1901** (forded the Orange near
Bethulie). At Maraisburg he divided his force (Scheepers and Fouché stayed on). *(Confidence: high
for the route and exit date. [7][8])*

**Scheepers (Dec 1900 – Oct 1901).** Commandant **Gideon Scheepers** entered the Cape **16 December
1900** as part of Kritzinger's commando, then ranged widely across the Eastern Cape / Cape Midlands —
the **Middelburg, Graaff-Reinet, Aberdeen, Cradock, Murraysburg and Willowmore** districts (and
further south to Uniondale, Oudtshoorn). He was captured **~10–12 October 1901 near Prince Albert**
(later tried and executed). *(Confidence: high. [8][9][10])*

**Kritzinger — second invasion (May–Jun 1901).** Kritzinger re-entered the Cape **16 May 1901**
(Wikipedia: 19 May, at Norvalspont — a ~3-day discrepancy) with ~500 reinforcements; his commando
**captured Jamestown on 2 June 1901** and operated through Steynsburg, Theebus, Molteno, Cradock,
Tarkastad and Middelburg. *(Confidence: medium — the route partly aggregates a three-way commando
split; Jamestown's capture date is firm. [7][8])*

**Smuts (Sep–Nov 1901).** **Jan Smuts** crossed into the Cape on **3 September 1901** and his commando
passed through the eastern Cape — near **Jansenville, heading toward Aberdeen** — in **November 1901**,
bypassing Jansenville because of its heavily-armed Town Guard fort. *(Confidence: medium — single
local-history source; date corrected to Nov 1901. [11])*

**The British counter — capture of Kritzinger.** The reactive British system is best illustrated by
Kritzinger's end: on **16 December 1901** he was **wounded and captured at Fransmanskop near Hanover**
while charging the **Noupoort–De Aar railway blockhouse line in daylight**, by the column of **Lt-Col
Beauchamp Doran** (physical capture credited to the 3rd Bn Grenadier Guards). *(Confidence: high.
[7][8])*

## 3a. The British counter-insurgency machine (1901–02)  *(added in second research pass)*

From **June 1901 Lt-Gen John French held supreme command of operations in the Cape Colony**, running
a system of **mobile flying columns backed by an expanding blockhouse and railway cordon**. The
columns hunted the commandos across the Midlands:
- **14 July 1901** — French sent **four columns (Crewe, Wyndham, Doran, Scobell)** into the Camdeboo
  after Scheepers; a laager near **Aberdeen** was surprised on 15 August (~30 prisoners). [12]
- **Col Eyre Crabbe's** Midlands column brushed **Fouché** (May) and **Kritzinger** (July), falling
  back on **Mortimer** on 21 July 1901. [15]
- **Col George Gorringe's** column **routed Kritzinger near Steynsburg** (Stormberg district) on
  **13 August 1901** (the Boer officers Erasmus and Cachet taken). [16]
*(Confidence: high. [12][15][16])*

**The destruction of Lötter — the model pursuit.** Commandant **Johannes "Hans" Lötter** split from
Kritzinger near Maraisburg (now Hofmeyr) about **March 1901** and raided the Cape Midlands (Bamboesberg,
Bankberg). After Lötter cut up a party of French's Scouts near **Bethesda Road (10 Aug)**, **Doran's
column** struck him at **Garstland Kloof near Cradock (2 Sept)**, and **Lt-Col Harry Scobell's column
destroyed the commando at Groenkloof near Petersburg on 5 September 1901** (~19 Boers killed, ~114
captured; British ~9 killed). Lötter was tried under martial law and **executed at Middelburg (Cape)
about 12 October 1901**. *(Confidence: high. [17][18][19]. Caveat: casualty figures AND the exact farm
name — Groenkloof vs Bouwershoek/Tandjesberg — are source-conflicted; the often-cited 11 October
execution date was refuted 0-3, 12 October is the likely date.)*

**Western-Cape epilogue (outside the Eastern-Cape focus, included for completeness).** **Manie
Maritz** raided the western Cape / Namaqualand (Vanrhynsdorp, 7 Aug 1901; the **Leliefontein mission
attack, 31 Jan 1902**). **Smuts**, with Commandant van Deventer, reached within sight of Cape Town
then turned north — Springbokfontein (1 Apr 1902), Concordia (4 Apr) — and **besieged Okiep** for
about a month until peace. *(Confidence: high. [20])*

## 3a-i. The local defence — Town Guards & District Mounted Troops  *(added in the guard-units audit)*

Most Eastern-Cape towns raised a **Town Guard** of armed townsmen, often backed by mounted **District
Mounted Troops (DMT)** and a small fort or blockhouse — the static layer beneath the mobile British
columns. Those now plotted (each a sub-unit under the "Town Guards & District Mounted Troops" filter):
- **Graaff-Reinet Town Guard** — 100 men under **Cmdt J. Gardner** (Adjutant **Capt F. Rubidge**),
  grown to 220 by March 1902 — the district's main loyalist garrison.
- **Jansenville Town Guard** — formed under **Capt Gould**, fort built **January 1901**; the
  **Jansenville DMT** later helped take Wynand Malan near Sheldon (27 May 1902).
- **Pearston Town Guard** — overwhelmed when Kritzinger surrounded and looted the village (3–6 Mar 1901).
- **Aberdeen Town Guard** — **repulsed Scheepers & Fouché** on 6 Mar 1901 (the relief by Parsons/Scobell
  followed).
- **Willowmore Town Guard** — **six killed** defending the village against Scheepers' second attack
  (1 Jun 1901; the subject of a dedicated SAMHS study).
- **Tarkastad Mounted Troops** — with **Maj H.T. Nickalls (17th Lancers)** attached; in action near
  **Vaal Vlei, 8 Oct 1901**.

Many more towns were fortified (Knysna, Port Edward/Van Stadens for Port Elizabeth, etc.) but lay
outside the Midlands focus or saw no combat. *(Confidence: high for the listed actions; the wider fort
roster is in SAMHS vol102rt. [29])*

## 3b. Van Reenen, and actions around Steynsburg & Tarkastad  *(added on request)*

**Cmdt G.H.P. van Reenen** was one of the Cape commando leaders (Conan Doyle lists him among the
chief leaders alongside Kritzinger, Scheepers, Malan, Lötter, Smuts, Maritz, etc.). Documented
movements: he led **~300 men back north across the Orange River about March 1901** (the first-invasion
retreat); on **7 June 1901** his camp at **Ruigtevlei, just south of Steynsburg**, was rushed at night
by the **17th Lancers under Col Wyndham** — betrayed by a loyalist farmer — with **~18 rebels captured**
(**Veldkornet Jan Kruger**, a Burgersdorp theology student, and a British soldier killed) while **van
Reenen and Capt Botha escaped**; five of the captured were sentenced to death at **Steynsburg on 17
June** (Kloppert, Andries & Christiaan Kruger, van Biljon, Koekemoer) — but **lots were drawn so that
only one would be shot as an example**: the lot fell on **Petrus Kloppert**, executed at **Burgersdorp
on 24 July 1901**, while **the other four (including both Krugers) were spared and exiled to Bermuda**.
*(Family record: **Jan Kruger and Christiaan Kruger were both veldkornets** — Boer field-cornets, an
elected junior-officer rank; Jan Kruger's death is confirmed by the family.)* And in
**early 1902** his was one of four commandos (Pypers, Smit, Hugo, Van Reenen) ordered under **Gen
Wynand Malan** back to the Midlands — though his band reportedly stayed in the north-west.
*(Confidence: medium. The Ruigtevlei action is corroborated three ways [12][14][22]; Conan Doyle dates
it 6 June with 22 prisoners while the Afrikaans account gives 7 June and 18 captured — the same
engagement, with the figures/date in conflict.)*

**Steynsburg** was a recurring flashpoint on the Stormberg–Noupoort frontier: Kritzinger crossed
through it on both invasions; **Gorringe routed Kritzinger near Steynsburg on 13 Aug 1901** and then
**pursued the commandos Steynsburg → Venterstad on 18 Aug, killing ~20**; the van Reenen/Wyndham
action (6 Jun) was here too. *(Confidence: high for the Gorringe actions [12][14]; medium for the
van Reenen action.)*

**Tarkastad** district saw the war's most famous Cape ambush: on **17 September 1901 Smuts's commando
(~250 men) overwhelmed C Squadron, 17th Lancers (Capt Victor Sandeman) at Modderfontein farm in the
Elands River Poort near Tarkastad** — a Boer victory (British **29 killed, 41 wounded, 60 captured**;
Boer **1 killed, 6 wounded**) from which Smuts re-armed his ragged men with captured British kit
(coords ≈ 31.85 S, 26.17 E). Kritzinger and Lötter had also ranged through the Tarkastad district
earlier in 1901. *(Confidence: high for Modderfontein. [21])*

The wider **17th Lancers disposition around Tarkastad** — previously missing from the chronology — is now
documented from the regimental account: C Squadron (Sandeman) **railed up from Stormberg on 15 September**
and was pushed forward to van Heerden's farm Modderfontein (~22 km NW of Tarkastad) to **guard the
Buysrivierpoort and Elandsrivierpoort** passes; **Lt-Col Douglas Haig held his headquarters and two more
Lancer squadrons at Tarkastad** (A Squadron camped forward at Hoogstede). When the attack message reached
Haig at **16:30 on 17 September he galloped the ~22 km to Modderfontein in about 75 minutes**, arriving too
late to pursue; the wounded were carried back through Tarkastad to the **Red Cross hospital at Cradock**.
*(Confidence: high. [26])*

## 3c. Family thread — the Kruger veldkornets  *(family + records research)*

*This section is researched at the family's request and mixes documentary sources with family record;
provenance is marked.*

- **Veldkornet Jan Kruger** — killed by the 17th Lancers at **Ruigtevlei (7 June 1901)**. Death
  confirmed by family (a headstone has been seen in person). A Burgersdorp theology student; note that a
  *second* theology student, **Calman (Callie) Lion-Cachet** (son of Prof. Jan Lion-Cachet), rode with
  the same commando and — per family record — once **cut a bullet out of Christiaan Kruger** before
  himself being killed in a skirmish at the farm **Ruiterskraal, also in the Steynsburg district**.
- **Veldkornet Christiaan Kruger** and **Andries Kruger** — captured at Ruigtevlei, condemned at
  Steynsburg (17 June), spared by the drawing of lots, and exiled.
- **Bermuda POW records — CONFIRMED (family-corroborated):**
  *Andries Jacob Kruger* (1873–1928), **Hawkins Camp, POW No. 21133**, and *Johannes Christiaan Kruger*
  (1875–1964), **Hawkins Camp, POW No. 21134**. The **consecutive POW numbers** (captured/processed
  together) and the fact that **Hawkins Island was specifically the camp for Cape rebels whose death
  sentences were commuted to penal servitude** (they worked the stone quarries) fit the two brothers —
  and **the family confirms these records (birth/death years 1873–1928 and 1875–1964) match the family
  tree.** (The POW database does not itself state the Steynsburg/Ruigtevlei capture; the link to the
  Ruigtevlei action rests on the family record plus the matching identities.)
- **The Cape-rebel exile arc** (general, well-attested — would apply to them): commuted life sentences
  served on **Hawkins Island, Bermuda**; a **January 1903 Royal Commission** reduced the sentences;
  rebels returned to South Africa aboard the **SS Sunda** in 1903, were held in **Tokai Gaol**, and were
  freed under the general amnesty of **22 March 1903**. [23][24]
